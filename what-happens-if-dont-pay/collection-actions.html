How we apply credits, benefits and other payments from the government to your debt

In some cases we automatically apply tax refunds and credit or benefit payments you are entitled to (such as the GST/HST credit) to tax and other government debts you owe. The type of payments we automatically apply to your debt depend on the type of debt you owe.

When we start collections actions

If you have a debt you cannot pay on time, the first thing to do is try to make an arrangement to pay your debt in smaller payments over time.

If you have a debt and have not contacted us to make arrangements to pay it, or if you do not make your scheduled payments, we may begin legal collections actions. Normally, we make several attempts to notify you verbally and in writing before we begin legal collections actions.

Corporate debts and COVID-19 business subsidy debts

For corporations, the notice of assessment or reassessment is considered the written legal warning for goods and services tax/harmonized sales tax (GST/HST) and payroll deduction amounts owing. Once a verbal legal warning is attempted, the CRA can proceed with collections actions. The CRA can continue to collect amounts assessed on corporate accounts even if you filed an objection or an appeal to the Tax Court.

If you are a large corporation, as defined in the Income Tax Act, and have filed an objection or an appeal, the CRA can start collecting 50% of your assessed income tax owing right away.

For corporate tax, GST/HST remittance, payroll, and COVID-19 business subsidy debts, set-offs can be issued as soon as a notice of determination/assessment has been sent.

If we determine there is a risk of not collecting all or part of your assessed corporate income tax or COVID-19 business subsidy overpayment debt while you are under collection restrictions, we can apply to the Federal Court or the superior court of a province for a jeopardy order. This allows the CRA to secure any assets that you own and to take immediate action to collect the debt.

Garnishing your income and accounts, and requesting amounts from third parties

In some cases when you have a debt you have not paid, we can legally ask someone who owes you money or holds money for you to send us that money. This is often your employer or a financial institution, but can also include a spouse, business partner, director, individual, corporation, or another government department.

  • Requirement to Pay (RTP)
  • Demand on Third Party (DTP)
  • Enhanced Requirement to Pay (ERTP) for payroll deductions and GST/HST debts
