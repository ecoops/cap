Making a payment to the CRA

If you don't pay

There are consequences if you don't pay your taxes or other debts when they are due:

  • interest will accumulate on your balance owing
  • penalties may be charged
  • some benefits and other government payments will be used to offset your debt
  • after providing warning, we will being legal collection actions

If you need help or need to make smaller payments over time, refer to: Unable to pay in full or on time

Interest and penalties you could be charged

The CRA charges interest on unpaid balances owing. In some cases, there might also be penalties associated with missing a payment deadline.

Offsets from benefits or other government payments

If you have a balance owing, we may automatically apply tax refunds and government credit or benefit payments you are entitled to (such as the GST/HST credit) to your tax and other government debts.

How we will notify you when we begin taking action

If you have a debt and have not contacted us to make arrangements to pay it, or if you do not make your scheduled payments, we may begin legal collections actions.

If you owe a debt with the CRA for more than 90 days after we notify you, CRA collections will generally:

  • make up to 3 attempts to give you a verbal legal warning by phone
  • send 1 written legal warning letter

However, if your business owes a payroll or GST/HST remittance debt, in some cases we may begin collections actions right after you are notified of your debt.

Business debts and COVID-19 business subsidy debts

For GST/HST remittance and payroll deduction debts, the notice of assessment or reassessment is considered the written legal warning for amounts owing. Once a verbal legal warning is attempted, the CRA can proceed with collections actions. The CRA can continue to collect amounts assessed on corporate accounts even if you filed an objection or an appeal to the Tax Court.

If you are a large corporation, as defined in the Income Tax Act, and have filed an objection or an appeal, the CRA can start collecting 50% of your assessed income tax owing right away.

For corporate tax, GST/HST remittance, payroll, and COVID-19 business subsidy debts, set-offs can be issued as soon as a notice of determination/assessment has been sent.

If we determine there is a risk of not collecting all or part of your assessed corporate income tax or COVID-19 business subsidy overpayment debt while you are under collection restrictions, we can apply to the Federal Court or the superior court of a province for a jeopardy order. This allows the CRA to secure any assets that you own and to take immediate action to collect the debt.

Make sure it's really the CRA

If you receive a call about a CRA balance owing that you never saw on a Notice of Assessment, Notice of Reassessment, or Notice of Determination:

  • verify the caller is really a CRA employee
  • check your balance on your online CRA account or by calling us

For more about what a CRA agent will and will do, refer to: What to expect when the CRA contacts you

Legal collection actions we may take

Depending on your situation, there are different legal actions the CRA might take.

  • Garnishing your income and accounts
  • Making another person or entity co-liable for your debt
  • Putting a lien on or seizing your assets

The CRA works within certain limitations, refer to: Time limits for how long we can collect your debt
